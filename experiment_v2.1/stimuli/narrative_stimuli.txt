**Narrative Stimuli**

*Linear, Nonlinear, and Atemporal Versions*

Eight story domains, each with three versions (linear, nonlinear, atemporal), totalling twenty-four stimuli.

Each event is confined to a single labelled paragraph (E1-E8). Prose within each event is held constant across versions where possible. Systematic differences are block order (linear: A-B-C-D; nonlinear and atemporal: C-B-A-D), tense (linear/nonlinear in past tense; atemporal in eternal present), and temporal connectives (present in linear/nonlinear, absent in atemporal).

*This is the full stimulus set. Six domains are used in both the behavioural and computational arms; two (care home incident, family conflict) are retained for the computational arm only and are excluded from the human battery.*

# Design Overview

## Event-to-paragraph mapping

Each of the eight events occupies exactly one paragraph, explicitly labelled with its event ID. Paragraph 1 is E1, paragraph 2 is E2, and so on through paragraph 8 (E8). This strict mapping ensures clean block boundaries across versions and grounds participants' event-level judgements in discrete, separable narrative units.

## Block structure

Events are grouped into four narrative blocks: A (E1, E2: setup), B (E3, E4: rising action), C (E5, E6: climax), D (E7, E8: resolution). This segmentation maps onto classical story structure theory (Freytag's pyramid) and cognitive narratology's recognition of psychologically real narrative phases.

**Linear version**

Paragraphs presented in chronological order: E1, E2, E3, E4, E5, E6, E7, E8.

## Nonlinear version (C-B-A-D permutation)

Blocks presented in C-B-A-D order, producing a paragraph reading sequence of E5, E6, E3, E4, E1, E2, E7, E8. Within-block chronological order is preserved. D held final across all conditions to keep story offset consistent.

## Atemporal version

Same block ordering as the nonlinear version (C-B-A-D), but rewritten in eternal present tense with all inter-paragraph temporal connectives removed. Events coexist rather than sequence. Tests whether weakening the temporal scaffold changes causal attribution in humans and models.

# Hospital Incident

*Topology: Convergent | Narrator: The nurse*

## Linear Version

**E1.** The hospital administrator circulated a memo announcing changes to overnight staffing levels. The new policy meant fewer nurses on the ward between midnight and six, and it was framed as a budget measure. No formal objections were raised.

**E2.** Around the same time, a contractor came in to service one of the ventilators on the respiratory ward. He needed to disable the alarm system to run his diagnostics, which was standard procedure. I saw him working on the machine during my afternoon shift.

**E3.** He packed up his tools and left that evening. The alarm system on the ventilator was not switched back on, and the machine sat in the corner of the bay, silent and unmonitored.

**E4.** A few days later I came on for a night shift and found my patient list longer than usual. The new staffing arrangement meant I was covering beds I wouldn't normally be responsible for, spread across two bays instead of one. I made my rounds, checked vitals, and updated charts.

**E5.** Sometime after two in the morning, the patient in bay three shifted in his sleep. The ventilator tube ran from his mask up to the machine on a swing arm, and the movement loosened the connection at the mask end. The seal broke and the tube partially detached.

**E6.** The ventilator continued cycling but was no longer delivering air to the patient. No alarm sounded.

**E7.** I was in the other bay at the time, attending to a patient who needed repositioning. It was perhaps twenty minutes before I came back through. I found the patient in bay three in respiratory distress, lips blue and oxygen saturation dangerously low. I hit the emergency call and began manual ventilation. The team arrived quickly and the patient was stabilised, but it had been close.

**E8.** In the weeks that followed, the hospital convened a formal inquest. They examined the staffing policy, the maintenance log, the tube fitting, and my movements that night. The contractor was called in to give a statement and the administrator attended as well. I gave my account as clearly as I could.

# Care Home Incident

*Topology: Convergent (structural replicate) | Narrator: The care assistant*

## Linear Version

**E1.** At the beginning of the month, the manager called the staff together and explained that the budget was tight. She was cutting back on overnight staffing, which meant the usual schedule of hourly room checks would be reduced to one at midnight and one at five.

**E2.** Around the same time, a visiting GP came to review one of the residents who had been on a strong sedative for several months. He examined her, decided the dose was too high for her current condition, and lowered it.

**E3.** He told me he would update the medication chart in her room before he left. I went to finish the afternoon round in the other corridor. The chart was not updated. The old dose, the higher one, was still written on the card when I came through that evening.

**E4.** With the reduced staffing, I was covering rooms that had previously been shared between two of us. I worked through the evening medication round for all the residents on the wing, checking each chart and giving the dose written there. I gave the resident the dose written on her card. It was the dose she had been on for months.

**E5.** Sometime during the night, the resident's condition began to worsen. The higher dose was too strong for her now more stable baseline. Her breathing slowed.

**E6.** I was not in her room to see the early signs. My midnight check covered the larger rooms first, and by the time I reached her room at the end of the corridor, things had already progressed.

**E7.** I found her unresponsive in bed, her breathing shallow and slow, her lips slightly blue. I rang the emergency line and began the basic resuscitation steps I had been trained in. The paramedics arrived within fifteen minutes and took over. She was stabilised at the hospital, though I was told another half hour might have made the difference.

**E8.** The manager called a meeting the following week. She brought in a regional inspector and asked me to walk through my evening routine. The medication chart was passed around the table and the staffing schedule was reviewed. Each of us described what we had done and when.

# Community Fair

*Topology: Three-thread convergence | Narrator: The organiser*

## Linear Version

**E1.** I booked an outdoor venue at the edge of the park for the annual community fair. It was a large open field with good access and enough space for forty stalls. The council approved the permit in early June and I began planning straight away.

**E2.** Over the following weeks I arranged the layout. Stalls along the perimeter, a children's area at the far end, and a shaded rest area near the entrance with benches and a water station. The council's volunteer coordination office assigned a first-aid team to cover the full day, and I confirmed their attendance the week before.

**E3.** On the morning of the fair, about an hour after the gates opened, the coordination office called the first-aid team away. There had been an incident at a sports event on the other side of town and they were the nearest available responders. I was told the reassignment was temporary. They packed up their equipment and left.

**E4.** I called the coordination office and asked how soon a replacement could arrive. They told me thirty minutes, but the nearest available team had to finish at another site first. Thirty minutes passed, then an hour. I called again and was told they were on their way. The gap stretched to nearly two hours.

**E5.** The day was hotter than anyone had expected. The forecast had predicted warm weather, but by midday the temperature was well above what had been announced. The field had little natural shade outside the rest area, and the ground reflected the heat back up.

**E6.** I opened the rest area earlier than planned and began handing out bottles of water to anyone who looked uncomfortable. Volunteers helped me carry extra cases from the storage tent, and we moved some of the stall canopies to create more shade along the main path.

**E7.** An elderly man near one of the stalls had been walking the fair since the gates opened. He had stopped at several stalls and was standing in direct sun by one of the food vans. He sat down on the grass and then slumped forward. A woman nearby called out and I ran over. He was conscious but barely responsive, his skin hot and dry, with the flushed look of someone who had overheated badly. There was no first-aid team on site, no equipment, and no shade where he had fallen. We moved him to the rest area and I called an ambulance. The replacement team arrived while we were waiting.

**E8.** The council convened a review the following week. They examined the venue booking, the volunteer scheduling, the weather conditions, and the sequence of events that morning. The coordination office provided their call logs. I presented my planning documents and described what had happened.

# Restaurant Fire

*Topology: Long chain | Narrator: The sous chef*

## Linear Version

**E1.** I had been complaining about the ventilation in our kitchen for over a year. On busy nights the air was thick enough to make your eyes sting. A health inspector visited the restaurant on a routine check and told the owner the system was not up to standard. The inspector gave the owner a deadline to fix it and moved on.

**E2.** The owner booked a repair. The deadline was tight and a busy weekend was coming up. Rather than waiting for the company that had originally installed the system, he called the first available technician.

**E3.** The technician came on the Friday morning and I let him in and showed him the kitchen. He spent the day up in the ceiling space and replaced the fan that pulls air through the system. While he was up there he also replaced a section of the pipe that carries gas to one of the burners. I asked him before he left whether everything was in order and he said it was. He had replaced the fan but not the old ducts behind the walls. The fan was strong enough to pass the inspection, but the rest of the system was the same one I had been working under for years.

**E4.** Within a few hours I started noticing a faint smell near the back burner, the one closest to the wall. It was hard to tell whether it was new. The kitchen always smelled of gas when all six burners were running. I mentioned it to the owner but neither of us followed up.

**E5.** That evening we were fully booked. I was running the pass, calling orders and plating. All six burners were going and the kitchen was loud, hot, and full of steam.

**E6.** A line cook lit the back burner under a saucepan and stepped away to check another station. The flame was barely up when there was a low whump from inside the wall. The wall ignited along the panel and the fire spread in seconds.

**E7.** The old ducts behind the walls carried the heat and smoke up through the ceiling rather than pulling it outside. The dining room filled with smoke within minutes. I shouted for everyone to get out and we evacuated through the back corridor. The customers were led out through the front.

**E8.** A fire investigation was conducted over the following weeks. The investigators examined the gas pipe, the ventilation system, and what happened that evening. The technician's work order was reviewed and the health inspector's original report was placed alongside the compliance sign-off. I gave my account of the smell I had noticed and the night of the fire.

# School Trip

*Topology: Fan-out | Narrator: The teacher*

## Linear Version

**E1.** I chose the mountain trail despite the uncertain weather forecast. The group was made up of high school students ranging from fifteen to eighteen years old, most of whom had hillwalking experience from previous trips. The route was one I had walked before, a ridge path that climbs above the valley and loops back through a wooded section on the far side. The forecast showed a chance of light rain later in the afternoon, but the morning was clear and the group was keen.

**E2.** We set out around nine. I checked the kit list before we left and noticed that several students had light jackets rather than proper waterproofs. I made a note of it but decided we would be back before conditions changed. We carried one emergency shelter between us and enough food and water for the day.

**E3.** The weather, which had been forecast to pick up hours later, came in sooner and much stronger than expected. By midday the ridge was in cloud and a cold rain was coming in sideways. I stopped the group at the high point and looked back along the trail we had come up. The route back was clear but exposed. The path forward looped down into the valley and through the trees. I decided to push on. The path, which had been dry that morning, was running with water, and the rocks along the exposed section became slick.

**E4.** One of my students lost footing on a wet rock and fell awkwardly. She grabbed at the slope but landed on her ankle. When I reached her she was sitting on the path holding her foot, unable to put weight on it. I could see the swelling starting almost immediately.

**E5.** We were on the far side of the ridge by then, deep in the valley. I took out my phone to call for help and found no signal. I tried moving to higher ground but the ridge was too exposed in the wind to stay on safely.

**E6.** I made the decision to split the group. I sent the two oldest and most experienced students back along the ridge path toward the car park, where I knew there was reception. I gave them the emergency shelter and told them to call mountain rescue and wait. The rest of us would stay with the injured student and move slowly down to the tree line.

**E7.** The remaining group waited under the trees. The rain did not let up. I had the injured student as close to the trunk of a large pine as I could manage, but the wind was cutting through. One of the younger students, who had been in a thin jacket since morning, began shivering badly and his responses became slow and confused. I recognised the early signs of hypothermia and wrapped him in every spare layer we had.

**E8.** A review board convened the following week to examine my decisions. The route choice, the kit check, the decision to push on rather than turn back at the high point, and the decision to split the group. I attended with my written account and answered their questions.

# Family Conflict

*Topology: Chain with feedback | Narrator: Adult sibling / caregiver*

## Linear Version

**E1.** My father was diagnosed with early-stage dementia in the spring. The doctor explained what to expect over the coming months and gave us a list of local support services. My sister and I sat in the car park afterwards and agreed that things would need to change.

**E2.** I moved closer to home that summer. I found a flat ten minutes from my father's house and began managing his daily routine. Meals, medication, appointments, bills. I tried to bring in outside help in the first few weeks. I arranged for a visiting carer twice a week and put his name down for a day centre. He refused both. He did not want strangers in the house and would not get in the car when the day centre minibus came. After a while I stopped trying. My sister lived further away but said she would come on weekends to give me a break.

**E3.** She came the first two weekends. After that the visits became unreliable. She would confirm on Thursday and cancel on Saturday morning. A work deadline, a friend's birthday, car trouble. Each time she said she would make it up the following week, and this continued through the autumn.

**E4.** I raised it with her one evening on the phone. I told her I was managing everything alone and that the weekend visits were the one thing I had asked for. She told me I was being controlling, that I had chosen to move closer and was now holding it over her, that she had a life too. The conversation ended badly.

**E5.** A few months later, my father's doctor recommended changing his medication. The current prescription was no longer managing his symptoms well enough, and there was an alternative worth trying. The decision needed to be made within the week.

**E6.** I authorised the change. I signed the form at the surgery and collected the new prescription that afternoon. I did not call my sister. I told myself there was a time constraint, that I was the one who attended every appointment, that I understood his condition better.

**E7.** My sister found out from a neighbour who had seen me at the pharmacy. She called me that evening and the conversation was short. She said I had cut her out of a decision about our father's health. I said she had cut herself out months ago.

**E8.** We met a few weeks later at a cafe near my father's house. We sat across from each other and tried to talk through what had happened.

# Power Cut

*Topology: Chain with critical distal enabler | Narrator: Resident / council member*

## Linear Version

**E1.** The council had been putting off tree maintenance near the power lines for over a year. Each quarter the budget review came around and each quarter the work was deferred. The trees along the lane behind our street had not been cut back since before I joined the community council.

**E2.** Earlier that year, a neighbour two doors down had been discharged from hospital with a serious respiratory condition that required continuous oxygen, day and night. The home healthcare provider delivered an oxygen concentrator and set it up in his front room, plugged into the wall. The provider had not supplied a backup battery unit with the machine. I remember him mentioning the equipment at a council meeting when he asked whether the street had ever experienced long outages. Someone told him it was rare.

**E3.** In late October an autumn storm moved in. The forecast warned of heavy winds overnight and into the following morning, and most of us secured outdoor furniture and stayed indoors.

**E4.** During the night, a large branch from one of the unmaintained trees came down across the power line that feeds our street. The weight of the rain and the force of the wind brought it down.

**E5.** The local transformer tripped and the street went dark. I was awake and heard the hum of the fridge stop. I assumed it would come back on within a few minutes, but it did not.

**E6.** My neighbour's oxygen machine shut down with the rest of the power. The machine had no internal battery and required a constant connection. I did not know this at the time.

**E7.** About an hour into the outage, I went to check on him. I knocked on his door and got no answer, so I tried the handle and found it unlocked. He was in his chair, unresponsive. I called emergency services immediately.

**E8.** The council convened an inquiry in the weeks that followed. The maintenance records for the trees along the lane were pulled, and the power company provided a timeline of the outage and the transformer fault. The home healthcare provider was asked to account for the equipment setup and the absence of a backup battery. The minutes from the budget meetings where the tree work had been deferred were read into the record.

# Missed Flight

*Topology: Hourglass | Narrator: The traveller*

## Linear Version

**E1.** That Tuesday morning, a truck jackknifed on the main highway heading toward the airport. The highway was the only quick route out to the terminal from where I lived, and traffic backed up for miles in both directions. I did not know about the accident when I left the house.

**E2.** Meanwhile, the airline's check-in system had gone offline overnight for scheduled maintenance. The maintenance window had been published in advance and the airline had emailed passengers the week before. The window was supposed to end well before the first departures of the day.

**E3.** I left the house later than I had planned. I had been up late preparing for a client meeting scheduled for that afternoon in another city, and I set my alarm for what I thought was enough time. I did not leave much margin, but it should have been sufficient on a normal day.

**E4.** I hit the traffic about twenty minutes from the airport and the highway was nearly at a standstill. I sat in the car watching the clock, calculating whether I could still make check-in. Every few minutes the traffic moved forward a car length and then stopped again. I could not reach the terminal in time.

**E5.** The airline's check-in system came back online later than expected. By the time the desks reopened, the window for my flight had already closed. The desks stayed dark through the early morning hours.

**E6.** I missed the flight. The next available seat was not until the following morning.

**E7.** I called the client from the airport to explain. I could hear the pause before they responded. They had rearranged their afternoon around the meeting, and I was not going to be there. The conversation was short and polite, but the tone had changed. I sat in the terminal afterwards, going over what I might have said differently, though there was nothing to say. I had missed the meeting.

**E8.** The following week, I learned that the client had signed with a competitor. I later found out the competitor had been in talks with them for several weeks before my trip was scheduled.
